Here is the background information about your tasks

Task 1

Fire and explosion of fuel tanks is one of the major reasons that cause aircraft accidents. The fuel/air mixture in the fuel tank vapor space (ullage) is susceptible to ignition during flight resulting from equipment malfunctions or external attacks. On July 21th, 2008, the FAA required operators and manufacturers to incorporate a Flammability Reduction Means (FRM) or ignition mitigation means on fuel tanks having a flammability exposure exceeding certain thresholds. These amendments affect new aircraft types, new aircraft in production and in-service aircraft produced after January 1st, 1992. The system is fitted as standard on-line fit aircraft today. The FAA were the first authority to propose a mandate for In Service aircraft and this was subsequently followed by other local authorities. The system conditions air from the engine bleed prior to passing it through an Inert Gas Generating System before entry into the aircraft Centre Fuel Tank.

Task 2

Because fire is one of the most dangerous threats to an aircraft, the potential fire zones of modern multi engine aircraft are protected by a fixed fire protection system. A fire zone is an area, or region, of an aircraft designed by the manufacturer to require fire detection and/or fire extinguishing equipment and a high degree of inherent fire resistance. The term “fixed” describes a permanently installed system in contrast to any type of portable fire extinguishing equipment, such as a hand-held Halon or water fire extinguisher. A complete fire protection system on modern aircraft, and on many older aircraft, includes a fire detection system and a fire extinguishing system. Automatic systems can detect aircraft fires or potential ignition which might not be apparent to the crew until they have developed to an extent which makes their successful control difficult, or impossible. These systems are based upon both heat and smoke sensing.

Here are your tasks for the day

Task 1

Task info:

The 319s and 320s are equipped with a fuel tank Inerting system. The intent of this system is to reduce flammability in the center fuel tank by producing oxygen-depleted air that goes in the center tank to replace the ambient air. This system extracts and conditions some engines to bleed air to adequate pressure and temperature. The air then goes through the air separation module, which produces oxygen-depleted air (with less than 12% oxygen), replacing the ambient air of the center tank. The fuel tank Inerting system does not require any pilot action. It works independently as soon as the engines start until they stop.

Task:

An Inerting system decreases the probability of combustion of flammable materials stored in a confined space, especially a fuel tank, by maintaining a chemically non-reactive or "inert" gas, such as nitrogen, in such a space. The Inerting systems on aircraft, this would run continuously to reduce fuel vapor flammability whenever the aircraft's engines are running; and its goal is to reduce oxygen content within the fuel tank to 12%, lower than normal atmospheric oxygen content of 21%. This is accomplished by ventilating fuel vapor laden ullage gas out of the tank and into the atmosphere. Let’s assume, you are in front of the interview board and trying to land your first job. Part of the fire protection system covered the Fuel Inerting system as fuel tank explosion is not new in the aviation industry. One of the engineers asked you why fuel Inerting is only done in the center tank [ref: A320F]?

Task 2

Task info:

The APU Fire detection system has two identical detection loops (A and B) mounted in parallel. Each loop has a single detector element. Both loops are monitored by a FDU. The FDU sends FIRE and FAULT signals to the FWC for display on ECAM. The guarded FIRE P/B switches give a FIRE indication and the means to isolate affected systems. When the FIRE P/B is released out, fuel, electrical power and bleed supply are cut off and the APU is immediately shut down. This also arms the extinguishing system. For the APU, there is just one fire extinguisher bottle. It is discharged by a single AGENT P/B switch. On the ground, an APU FIRE will trigger an APU automatic shutdown and discharge the bottle automatically. The TEST button is used to test the fire detection and extinguishing system operation.

Task:

Pneumatic detectors are based on the principles of gas laws. The sensing element consists of a closed helium-filled tube connected at one end to a responder assembly. As the element is heated, the gas pressure inside the tube increases until the alarm threshold is reached. At this point, an internal switch closes and reports an alarm to the cockpit. The pneumatic detector integrity pressure switch opens and triggers the fault alarm if the pneumatic detector loses pressure, as in the case of a leak.
